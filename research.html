Research

Three pillars of a machine-oriented programme

What symbolic methods look like when they are designed for machine consumption rather than human readability.

⊢

Pillar I · Constraining · Boosting · Auditing

Symbolic checks on neural systems

New-fashioned AI should be auditable by symbolic means. The central question is how to design interactive theorem provers (ITPs) that are LLM-friendly — and, more radically, what ITPs look like when they are designed for machines rather than for humans. Current tools such as Lean and Agda make many things implicit that could be made explicit; unlike human mathematicians, LLMs prefer explicitness, as any prompt engineer knows.

Proofs in classical systems are binary: a proof or not a proof. But partial proofs are informative. They can be reused as heuristics, guiding further search even when they fall short of completeness. A mathematical theory of heuristics — partial proofs as informative objects, not failures — is one of the institute's core research targets.

⊨

Pillar II · World Models

Logic as bedrock for how LLMs represent the world

LLMs lack grounded world models. Symbolic ontologies like SUMO exist but are computationally intractable at scale. The institute investigates a different approach: world models that are not fully specified, but governed by admissibility. Rather than asserting what is true, the system specifies what facts are admissible — and only generates the next fact if it passes that test.

The insight is structural: just as fixing a grammar produces syntactically valid output, fixing an admissibility relation produces logically valid output. Deductions become a grammar for generation. Hardware-accelerated categorical structures and the formal work of David Pym on deductive and reductive reasoning provide the theoretical scaffolding.

□

Pillar III · GOFAI is not done

Classical AI, redesigned from first principles

The premise: symbolic AI was not refuted. It was outpaced — and then forgotten. The institute asks whether practitioners would accept Herbrand logic as a practical foundation for modern applications, if the tooling were redesigned for machine consumption. The wager is yes.

This means building systems that make explicit what Lean and Agda leave implicit; that treat the binary pass/fail of proof verification as a starting point rather than an endpoint; and that ask, seriously, what GOFAI looks like when its primary user is not a human mathematician but a language model.
