Mission & manifesto

Three pillars of a machine-oriented programme

These three pillars are the institute's mission — a manifesto for what symbolic methods can be when they are designed for machine consumption rather than human readability.

Trustworthy AI (TAI), Responsible AI (RAI), and Explainable AI (XAI) set the scene. We are wary of the labels — but the concern behind them is real, and we think logic, not a compliance layer bolted on at the end, is where the answer lies.

⊢

Pillar I · Constraining · Boosting · Auditing

Symbolic checks on neural systems

New-fashioned AI should be checkable — and, where possible, checked — by symbolic means: methods that constrain a model's output, boost its reasoning, and audit its results. Interactive theorem provers (ITPs) are the natural instrument, but tools like Lean and Agda were built for human mathematicians and leave a great deal implicit that could be made explicit. LLMs are not human; as any prompt engineer will attest, they do better when things are spelled out. A second thread concerns proof search itself: a classical proof either is or is not a proof, yet incomplete proofs are often informative. A mathematical theory of heuristics would let partial proofs be reused as guidance — a way to read the state of play even when no complete proof is in hand.

Research questions

  • How can interactive theorem provers be designed to be LLM-friendly?
  • What does an ITP look like when designed for machines — making explicit what Lean and Agda leave implicit?
  • Can partial proofs be given rigorous status as heuristics that guide further search rather than count as failures?
  • How do we turn a stuck or partial proof into actionable guidance for a neural system?
⊨

Pillar II · World Models

Logic as a bedrock for world models

LLMs need a picture of the world. Hand-built ontologies such as SUMO supply one, but they are inefficient and do not scale. We pursue world models built with traditional logical techniques yet deliberately left not fully specified — governed instead by a notion of admissibility. The analogy is grammar: an LLM's output is already passed through a grammar checker to ensure it is well-formed, so why not a logic checker that emits the next fact only when it is admissible? Just as fixing a grammar produces syntactically valid output, fixing an admissibility relation produces logically valid output.

Research questions

  • What is the right notion of admissibility for a world model that is not fully specified?
  • Could a logic checker sit beside the grammar checker, admitting only logically valid next facts?
  • How do we capture the coverage of ontologies like SUMO without their intractability?
  • Which classical techniques transfer to specifying world models by admissibility rather than enumeration?
□

Pillar III · GOFAI is not done

Classical AI, continued — and built for machines

Symbolic AI was never refuted; within its remit it does exactly what it was built to do — and it is not finished. Continuing it means two things: taking mature classical results seriously as foundations for real applications, and redesigning the tooling for machine users rather than human ones. Where today's automated tools get stuck, the aim is to give them a hint, not to start over. A particular target is a mature theory of reductive logic — reasoning backwards from goals — to stand alongside the deductive tradition.

Research questions

  • Would practitioners accept Herbrand logic as a practical basis for applications?
  • When automated tools get stuck, how can we give them a productive hint?
  • What does GOFAI look like redesigned for LLM use rather than human use?
  • Can we develop a mature theory of reductive — rather than deductive — logic?
