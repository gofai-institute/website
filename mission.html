Mission & manifesto

Three pillars of a machine-oriented programme

These three pillars are our mission — and our manifesto for what symbolic methods can be when they are built for machines to use, not people to read.

Trustworthy AI (TAI), Responsible AI (RAI), and Explainable AI (XAI) set the scene. We are wary of the labels — but the worry behind them is real, and we think the answer is logic, not a layer of compliance bolted on at the end.

⊢

Pillar I · Constraining · Boosting · Auditing

Symbolic checks on neural systems

Today's AI should be open to checking by symbolic means — methods that constrain what a model can output, boost its reasoning, and audit its results. The natural tool is the interactive theorem prover (ITP): software that checks a proof step by step. But the leading systems, such as Lean and Agda, were built for human mathematicians, and leave much unsaid that could be made explicit. Large language models are not human, and — as any prompt engineer knows — they do better when everything is spelled out. A second thread is about proof search. In classical logic a proof either works or it does not, yet a half-finished proof can still be informative. A mathematical theory of heuristics would let these partial proofs be reused as guidance — a way to see where things stand even when no complete proof is in hand.

Research questions

  • How can interactive theorem provers be designed to be LLM-friendly?
  • What does an ITP look like when designed for machines — making explicit what Lean and Agda leave implicit?
  • Can partial proofs be given rigorous status as heuristics that guide further search rather than count as failures?
  • How do we turn a stuck or partial proof into actionable guidance for a neural system?

Pillar II · World Models

Logic as a bedrock for world models

A language model needs some picture of the world. Hand-built knowledge bases such as SUMO offer one, but they are slow and do not scale. We take a different route: world models built with traditional logical techniques but left deliberately open rather than fully pinned down — governed instead by what we call admissibility. Think of grammar. A model's output is already run through a grammar checker to make sure it is well formed; why not add a logic checker that allows the next fact only when it is admissible? Fixing a grammar gives you output that is grammatically valid; fixing a rule of admissibility gives you output that is logically valid.

Research questions

  • What is the right notion of admissibility for a world model that is not fully specified?
  • Could a logic checker sit beside the grammar checker, admitting only logically valid next facts?
  • How do we capture the coverage of ontologies like SUMO without their intractability?
  • Which classical techniques transfer to specifying world models by admissibility rather than enumeration?
□

Pillar III · GOFAI is not done

Classical AI, continued — and built for machines

Symbolic AI was never refuted. Within the job it was built for it works exactly as intended — and that job is far from finished. Continuing it means two things: treating well-established classical results as real foundations for applications, and rebuilding the tools for machines to use rather than people. When today's automated tools get stuck, our aim is to give them a hint, not to start from scratch. One specific goal is a mature theory of reductive logic — working backwards from a goal to the steps that would establish it — to sit alongside the usual forward, deductive style.

Research questions

  • Would practitioners accept Herbrand logic as a practical basis for applications?
  • When automated tools get stuck, how can we give them a productive hint?
  • What does GOFAI look like redesigned for LLM use rather than human use?
  • Can we develop a mature theory of reductive — rather than deductive — logic?
